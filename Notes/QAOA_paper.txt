The paper introduces the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA), a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm for approximate solutions to combinatorial optimization problems. These problems involve n bits and m clauses (constraints), with the objective function C(z) counting satisfied clauses for a bit string z. The goal is to find z maximizing C(z) (or get close to it).
Algorithm Structure (p-level QAOA)
Start with the uniform superposition state |s⟩ = (1/√2ⁿ) Σ |z⟩ (all possible bit strings equally likely).
Apply p layers of alternating unitaries:

U(C, γ) = e^{-iγC} — phase separation based on the objective (diagonal in computational basis; locality matches the problem's clauses).
U(B, β) = e^{-iβB}, where B = Σ σˣⱼ (mixer; flips bits).

The state is:
|γ, β⟩ = U(B, βₚ) U(C, γₚ) ⋯ U(B, β₁) U(C, γ₁) |s⟩
Measure in the computational basis to sample bit strings z, evaluate C(z). The expectation Fₚ(γ, β) = ⟨γ, β| C |γ, β⟩ improves with larger p, and lim_{p→∞} Mₚ = max C(z), where Mₚ is the maximum of Fₚ over angles.
Circuit depth is O(mp + p), with locality tied to the problem.
Key Theoretical Results & Techniques
Fixed-p Regime (Efficient Classical Preprocessing):

For problems like MaxCut on bounded-degree graphs, Fₚ can be computed classically without scaling with n.
Each term in the expectation depends only on a local subgraph of radius p around each edge/clause (tree size bounded by degree and p).
Group edges by isomorphic subgraph types, compute contributions f_g(γ, β) on small Hilbert spaces (dim ≤ 2^{q_tree}), weight by occurrence counts w_g.
Optimize angles classically, then run quantum circuit once (or few times) with optimal angles. Repeated measurements concentrate around the mean due to low variance (std dev O(√m) for fixed p/degree).

Performance Guarantees:

2-regular graphs (rings): For any p, Mₚ = n(2p+1)/(2p+2). Approximation ratio → 1 as p increases (independent of n). Circuit depth can be made independent of n.
3-regular MaxCut (p=1): Worst-case approximation ratio 0.6924 (better than random, but below best classical at the time). Proven by analyzing subgraph types (isolated triangles, crossed squares, etc.) and minimizing the ratio over possible graph compositions.
For p=2, partial results show ~0.7559 on typical large subgraphs.

Concentration: Variance analysis shows outputs concentrate near Fₚ, enabling high-probability good solutions with O(m log m) repetitions.
Relation to Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm (QAA):

QAOA is a Trotterized, fixed-p version of adiabatic evolution but optimized for approximation, not exact solution.
As p → ∞, QAOA can approximate the optimum, but overlap with the exact ground state may be tiny (e.g., on rings at p=1).
QAOA can succeed where QAA gets stuck (example given with symmetric objective).

Variant for Independent Sets:

Restricts Hilbert space to valid independent sets.
Starts from empty set |0⟩.
Uses adjacency-matrix mixer B on the valid subspace.
Similar alternating evolution, with guarantees via adiabatic paths.

Strategies for Angle Selection

Fixed p + bounded degree → classical optimization.
Grid search over angles (polynomial points for fixed p).
p growing with n → classical optimizers using quantum evaluations of Fₚ as subroutine, or problem-specific heuristics.

Strengths & Outlook

Fully quantum circuit with shallow depth for small p.
Approximation quality tunable via p.
Efficient classical pre-processing for many graphs.
Opens door to quantum advantage in approximation algorithms.

The paper is foundational—it defined QAOA, which became central to NISQ-era quantum optimization research (e.g., via variational quantum algorithms).


Comparison to Other Quantum Approaches
QAOA is related to the Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm (which tries to find the exact best solution slowly), but it’s more flexible and focused on getting good approximate answers faster. Interestingly, there are cases where QAOA succeeds even when the adiabatic method gets stuck.
Why It Matters
QAOA was groundbreaking because:

It works with relatively shallow circuits (good for near-term quantum hardware).
The quality is tunable — just increase p.
It combines classical smarts with quantum power elegantly.
It kicked off the whole field of variational quantum algorithms that dominate NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) research today.

In short, the paper presents a beautiful, practical, and theoretically grounded way to harness quantum computers for hard optimization problems — not by promising magic perfect solutions, but by delivering reliably good ones that improve with more quantum resources.
